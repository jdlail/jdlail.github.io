I haven't written about journalism uses of Amazon's [](https://www.mturk.com/ "Mechanical Turk") or mTurk [since 2008](/2008/10/journalism-on-the-mturk.html) when I read something from Andy Baio and I don't think I have read much about its use by journalists in the intervening time,  
  
But here's Amanda Michel of ProPublica detailing how that news organization [has been using mTurk](https://www.propublica.org/article/how-we-use-mechanical-turk-to-do-data-driven-reporting-and-how-you-can-too) complete with a [guide for journalists](https://www.propublica.org/article/propublicas-guide-to-mechanical-turk) written by her and Srinivas Rao. There also is [an example](https://www.propublica.org/special/mechanical-turk-hit-template-example) of a Human Intelligence Tasks request template.  
  
For those interested, Baio has written a [few posts](https://waxy.org/2008/11/the_faces_of_mechanical_turk/) on using mTurk for data research projects including [audio transcription](https://waxy.org/2008/09/audio_transcription_with_mechanical_turk/)and [data entry](https://waxy.org/2008/09/girl_turk/).  
  
Anybody else using it and what tasks did you use it for? Does the quality meet your standards? Other thoughts about it? Seems economical if the results aren't crap.Â  [Dan Kennedy](https://www.dankennedy.net/2010/07/16/crowdsourcing-the-pain-of-transcribing-audio/) wrote about his experience earlier this year.  
  
Here a snippet from Michel's piece.  
  
> For those unfamiliar with Mechanical Turk, it's an online marketplace, set up by the online shopping site Amazon, where anyone can hire workers to complete short, simple tasks like [quickly transcribing interviews](https://waxy.org/2008/09/audio_transcription_with_mechanical_turk/), [copying data from thousands of charts](https://glenmcgregor.tumblr.com/post/1167624539/mechanical-turk-vs-the-eqao), and [even sorting through satellite images in hopes of locating missing individuals](https://aws.typepad.com/aws/2007/02/help_find_jim_g.html). Amazon originally developed it as an in-house tool, and commercialized it in 2005. The mTurk workforce now numbers more than 100,000 workers in 200 countries.  
>
> At the urging of [Panos Ipeirotis](https://pages.stern.nyu.edu/%7Epanos/), a professor at New York University's Stern School of Business, we began experimenting with mTurk last spring to clean, de-duplicate and reformat data. We've since used the tool to collect or proof more than 28,000 data points, from the names of [companies that received stimulus money](https://projects.propublica.org/recovery) to the categorization of answers to our [home loan modification questionnaire](https://www.propublica.org/article/homeowner-questionnaire-shows-banks-violating-govt-program-rules). We're impressed with the speed and accuracy of its results. For example, a project we estimated would take a full-time staffer almost three days to finish was completed on mTurk overnight for $37, with 99 percent accuracy.  
>
> Mechanical Turk has proven to be more than a shortcut. It has freed up staff time for more complicated work. We've also used it to retrieve data from government databases that prohibit scraping.  
>
